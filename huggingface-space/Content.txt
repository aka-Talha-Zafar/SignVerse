Answer 1 — Is it possible?
Yes, definitively. Here is why the vocabulary is sufficient despite missing obvious words like "want" and "need":

ASL grammar is topic-comment based, not subject-verb-object. A native ASL sentence for "I want water" is more naturally signed as THIRSTY GIVE WATER PLEASE or simply WATER PLEASE — and every one of those words is in your 250-class dataset. You don't need "want" or "need" because the language structure doesn't require them. Common daily sentences like MOM GIVE MILK, MINEMY HUNGRY FOOD PLEASE, WE GO HOME NOW, HESHEIT SICK SLEEP, PLEASE GIVE WATER are all fully constructible from your existing vocabulary. You realistically have the ingredients for 200+ distinct meaningful sentences.

The one honest limitation: you cannot do continuous unrestricted signing. Someone cannot sign freely and expect arbitrary sentences. The system works within the vocabulary boundary — but for a daily communication aid (which is SignVerse's goal), that boundary is exactly where the most useful sentences live anyway.

Answer 2 — The exact pipeline, step by step
Stage 1 — Sign boundary detection (the only truly new ML piece)
This is the critical new component. You need to split a continuous stream of landmarks into individual word segments. The approach that works without new training data:

Compute the wrist velocity at each frame — the Euclidean distance between the right-hand wrist position (features[0:3]) in frame t and frame t-1. When a signer finishes one sign and prepares for the next, the hands briefly return to a neutral "rest position" near the hips, and velocity drops to near-zero. Threshold this: if velocity stays below 0.03 (in normalized wrist-relative coordinates) for more than 4 consecutive frames, mark a boundary. This gives you a clean segment per word with no additional training required.

As a fallback for signs that don't have a clear rest (like fast continuous signing), also use a fixed sliding window of 64 frames with 32-frame stride, run the classifier on each window, and apply temporal NMS to collapse repeated adjacent predictions into one.

Stage 2 — Run the existing classifier, keep top-3
This is your existing model, completely frozen and unchanged. The only difference: instead of returning just the top-1 prediction, you return the top-3 predictions plus their softmax confidence scores for each detected segment. This gives the downstream language model room to correct low-confidence word-level mistakes.

Stage 3 — Temporal deduplication
Because the classifier runs on overlapping windows, you'll often see the same word predicted 3–5 times in a row for a single sign. Collapse consecutive identical top-1 predictions into a single word. Keep only the instance with the highest confidence score. This is a simple 4-line function — not ML, just logic.

Stage 4 — Language model refinement (the intelligence layer)
This is where BERT fits in, and you have two options depending on how much time you want to spend:

Option A — Bigram language model (1 day of work, works immediately). Build a lookup table of bigram and trigram probabilities from a handcrafted corpus of ~500 ASL sentences composed from your 250-word vocabulary. Given the sequence of top-3 candidates per word slot, use Viterbi decoding to find the most likely word sequence given both the classifier confidence and the language model probability. This corrects mistakes like DUCK being predicted when DRINK was signed, because THIRSTY → DRINK → WATER has much higher bigram probability than THIRSTY → DUCK → WATER. The corpus takes a few hours to write; the model trains in seconds.

Option B — Fine-tuned BERT MLM (3–4 days of work, more robust). Take bert-base-uncased and fine-tune it on masked sentences composed entirely from your 250-word vocabulary. Because the vocabulary is tiny, you constrain BERT's output head to only consider tokens that map to your 250 ASL words — this is called a constrained vocabulary mask applied at the logits layer before softmax. During inference, you fill in the sentence with your top-1 word classifier predictions, randomly mask the lowest-confidence word, let BERT predict what it should be, and iterate 2–3 times. This is exactly masked language model correction and it handles multi-word context rather than just pairwise bigrams.

For your FYP deadline, Option A is the right call. It's demonstrably correct, explainable to your supervisor, and produces clean results. Option B is what you'd add in a post-submission sprint.

Stage 5 — Synthetic training data (optional but powerful)
One additional technique that costs almost nothing: since you have isolated clips for all 250 classes in the Google dataset, you can synthetically construct sentence-level training examples by concatenating .npy arrays. Take the cached .npy files you already generated (cache_v5/), pick 3–4 words that form a valid sentence, concatenate their arrays with a short zero-padding "rest" segment between them (8–12 zero frames to simulate the inter-sign gap), and save it as a sentence-level sample. You can generate thousands of synthetic sentence examples in an afternoon. These can then be used to fine-tune a small sequence model on top of your frozen word classifier if you want to add a proper CTC or attention decoder layer later.

Practical recommendation for your timeline
Given you just hit your submission deadline, the path is: implement Stage 1 (boundary detection) and Stage 3 (deduplication) in your FastAPI backend this week — they're pure Python, no training. Then use Option A (bigram LM) to add refinement. This gets you to a working sentence-level demo very quickly. The classifier was trained on isolated 64-frame segments of single signs — that's its expectation. Violating it produces nonsense.
But the model itself does not need to change at all. The solution is entirely about how you feed data to it. The boundary detection layer cuts the continuous stream into individual word segments before the classifier ever sees them — so from the model's perspective, it's still doing exactly what it was trained to do: classify one isolated sign at a time.

The one thing that changes your intuition: where the 4–5 seconds actually come from
Your current system works like this: camera runs, frames accumulate into a buffer, once you have 64 frames the buffer is sent to the model, result is displayed. That buffer-filling step is what takes 2+ seconds. The model inference itself takes under 80ms — it's nearly instantaneous.
In a sentence-level system you don't wait to collect frames first. Instead you run MediaPipe continuously in real time, detect the boundary the moment a sign ends (which you know from the velocity drop), extract the 64-frame window backward from that boundary, and run inference on it immediately. The classification happens in the same moment the signer begins their micro-pause. By the time they start the next sign, the previous word is already classified and sitting in the word buffer. There is genuinely no added latency.

The concrete implementation for Approach A (what to build for your FYP)
The boundary detector is about 30 lines of Python. You maintain a rolling buffer of the last 80 frames of MediaPipe output. At each new frame you compute velocity = norm(wrist_pos[t] - wrist_pos[t-1]). You maintain a counter of how many consecutive frames have had velocity below 0.03. When that counter crosses 4, you have a boundary — you slice the buffer from frame boundary_start to boundary_end, resample it to 64 frames using your existing interpolation code from asl_v5.py, apply normalize_landmarks(), and fire an async inference call. The result comes back in under 100ms and gets appended to the sentence buffer.
The sentence buffer holds the top-3 predictions and confidence scores for each detected word. When the user signals end-of-sentence (a timeout of 2 seconds with no new signs, or a button press), you run the bigram LM over the buffer to pick the best-scoring word sequence and display the sentence.
The bigram LM itself is just a Python dictionary of {(word_a, word_b): probability} entries estimated from a few hundred hand-written ASL-grammar sentences using your 250-word vocabulary. The Viterbi decoding over 3–4 words with top-3 candidates per slot runs in microseconds.
